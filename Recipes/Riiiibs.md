Riiiibs
=======

Dry-rubbed, ginger beer braised, glazed pork ribs; Ooohhhh yyyeeeaaaahhhh!

Ribs
----
Back ribs are meatier and cook more quickly, but country or st. louis spareribs work too, they just take longer to be as tender.  I always make at least two racks, since it isn't really any more work; then I vacuum-seal and freeze any extra.

Rib rub
-------
Per-rack:
- 75g brown sugar (½ C)
- 10g kosher salt (1½ tsp)
- 3g chili powder (1 tsp)
- 2g [beau monde](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=beau+monde+seasoning) (½ tsp) 
- ~1.2g [herbs de provence](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=morton+and+bassett+herbs+de+provence) (1½ tsp)
- 1g Chinese five spice (½ tsp)
- 1g ground coffee (½ tsp)

(Volumetric measures approximate; I recommend a kitchen scale, they’re inexpensive, much more accurate, and much more convenient than fussing with measuring cups and spoons.)

Braise
------
Per-rack:
- ~6oz good, spicy, ginger beer  (Bundaberg, Reed's Extra Spicy, Gosling's)

Glaze
-----
Per-rack:
- liquid from ribs
- ~1/4 C BBQ sauce (I like Stubbs Original)
- ~2 Tbsp hoisin sauce (note: contains soy)

The night before, rub each rack and wrap in extra-wide heavy-duty foil; refrigerate overnight.

Plan on 4 hours of cooking time or more; when in doubt, start early, the ribs can rest after brasing as needed and the finishing broil will ensure they go to the table hot.

To braise: lay the foil packets on a sheet pan or pans; open one end, pour in the ginger beer, and close again.  Slide into a 250°F oven, rotate every hour or two.  After 2-3 hours, you can start checking for tenderness: carefully peel back the foil, grasp the end of a bone with tongs and try to twist; if it's still firm, they need more time.  If it twists a bit, the ribs will still be a little chewy, but if you want to finish on a grill, they need to still hold together at least a bit.  If it rotates easily, they will be fall-off-the-bone tender.  If they need more time to get where you want them, button the foil back up and keep cooking.

When the ribs are tender, open one end of each packet and pour the liquid into a wide flat pan on the stove; keep the ribs wrapped while you make the glaze.

Start simmering the liquid from the ribs and stir in BBQ and hoisin sauce.  Keep reducing the liquid until it is a thick syrup, less than half the volume; the thicker the better, but don't burn it, there's a lot of sugar in there.

Open up the foil, but use it to keep the pans easy to clean; spoon and brush the glaze on the meat side and roast under the broiler or on the grill to carmelize.  WATCH CLOSELY, they can burn very quickly.  Repeat on the bone side and again on the meat side.

Serve with remaining glaze and more BBQ sauce.
